Academic text samples can be found in resources like Scribd documents and university writing centers to help you understand formats such as essays, research papers, and reports. These texts are characterized by formal language, specialized vocabulary, precise phrasing, and strict adherence to citation and referencing guidelines to present objective, evidence-based arguments. You can also find samples for specific academic skills like literature reviews, annotated bibliographies, and research proposals.
 
Where to Find Samples
University Writing Centers: Many university websites, like The University of Sydney, offer resources and examples of various academic writing types. 
Online Repositories: Scribd provides a wide range of sample academic texts and writing guides. 
Academic Publishers and Databases: Databases like those from EBSCO can offer access to published academic journals and articles, serving as examples of professional academic writing. 
Common Types of Academic Text Samples
Essays:
Short, focused pieces that present an argument or analysis on a specific topic. 
Research Papers:
In-depth studies of a research problem, including methods, results, and discussions, often following an IM RAD (Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion) structure. 
Lab Reports:
A structured document detailing an experiment, its findings, and interpretations. 
Literature Reviews:
A comprehensive summary and critical analysis of existing research on a particular subject. 
Research Proposals:
A document outlining a proposed research project, including the research question, methodology, and expected outcomes. 
Key Characteristics of Academic Texts
Formality:
Use of formal language and specialized vocabulary appropriate for a specific academic discipline. 
Objectivity:
Presenting information factually, based on evidence rather than personal opinion. 
Precision:
Using clear, accurate, and specific language to avoid ambiguity. 
Citations:
Strict adherence to academic citation and referencing styles, such as APA, MLA, or Chicago, to give credit to sources. 
Structure:
Adherence to common academic structures, like the IMRAD structure for scientific papers, for clarity and organization.
